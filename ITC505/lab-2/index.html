The Role of Participatory Village Maps in Strengthening Public Participation Practice

Authors (source): Aulia Akbar, Johannes Flacke, Javier Martinez, Martin F. A. M. van Maarseveen

Source (official): ISPRS Int. J. Geo-Inf. (2021) — DOI: 10.3390/ijgi10080512

Abstract

This study examines how participatory village maps produced with community workshops can improve public participation processes. The authors evaluate participatory mapping activities in several villages and analyze how the resulting maps informed local planning forums (Musrenbang) and contributed to better communication between residents and planners.

1. Introduction

Participatory mapping is a community-driven technique that captures local geographic knowledge and priorities. It can be used to represent boundaries, resources, social places, and local concerns that are often absent from formal datasets. This paper investigates whether village maps created through participatory workshops strengthen the local planning consultation (Musrenbang) in Indonesia.

2. Background

Prior research on participatory mapping and volunteered geographic information (VGI) shows benefits for local empowerment and planning. The authors situate their study within this literature and emphasize methodological attention to community representation and map co-production.

3. Methods

The study used participatory mapping workshops in five villages, followed by evaluation of Musrenbang meetings. Methods included group mapping exercises, focus discussions, and interviews with local stakeholders to assess how maps were used in the consultation process.

3.1 Participatory Mapping Workshops

Workshops guided residents to draw and annotate village features on base maps, identify priorities, and record changes or concerns. Workshop outputs were digitized and shared with local planning actors.

3.2 Evaluation of Musrenbang Use

The authors observed local Musrenbang meetings and interviewed participants to determine whether and how the participatory maps influenced discussion, decision making, and the legitimacy of proposed plans.

4. Results

The participatory village maps influenced local consultation in multiple ways: they helped residents communicate local priorities more clearly, provided concrete visual evidence during Musrenbang, and in some cases guided local planning choices. The study reports examples across the villages where maps were referenced and used to support requests or improvements in local proposals.

  • Maps made local issues visible to planners
  • Residents used the maps to advocate for specific infrastructure or services
  • Maps contributed to a shared understanding between stakeholders

5. Discussion

The study highlights that participatory mapping can strengthen participatory practice when outputs are recognized and integrated into official forums. The authors note the importance of follow-up, documentation, and respectful treatment of local knowledge to maintain trust and continued engagement.

6. Conclusion

Participatory village maps are a practical tool to improve public participation practice. The paper recommends that practitioners ensure outputs are visible in local decision-making processes, and that mapping activities are inclusive and well-documented so they become an ongoing resource for planning.
